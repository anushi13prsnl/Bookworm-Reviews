Book Reviews




1. A Good Girl's Guide to Murder

~ Holly Jackson

★★★★★

A Good Girl's Guide to Murder by Holly Jackson is a gripping YA mystery told in a unique format. You follow Pip, a determined student, as she reinvestigates the unsolved murder of Andie Bell through interviews, social media posts, and diary entries. The story is fast-paced and full of twists, keeping you guessing until the very end. Pip is a relatable protagonist, and the exploration of social media's role in the case adds a modern twist to the classic whodunit. If you enjoy mysteries with strong female leads and a touch of true crime intrigue, A Good Girl's Guide to Murder is a must-read. 







2. To Kill a Mockingbird

~ Harper Lee

★★★★☆

To Kill a Mockingbird by Harper Lee is a timeless coming-of-age story set against the backdrop of racial prejudice in the American South. Scout Finch, a curious and spunky young girl, narrates the tale as she and her brother Jem grapple with complex issues of justice and morality. Lee's masterful use of Scout's innocent perspective makes the novel both endearing and thought-provoking. The characters are unforgettable, particularly Atticus Finch, Scout's lawyer father, who embodies integrity and courage in the face of overwhelming odds. While some criticize the book's portrayal of race relations, To Kill a Mockingbird remains a powerful exploration of good versus evil, with enduring lessons about empathy and the importance of fighting for what's right. Highly recommended for anyone seeking a beautifully written story with timeless themes. 







3. Jane Eyre

~ Charlotte Brontë

★★★★☆

Jane Eyre by Charlotte Brontë is a gothic romance that transcends its genre. Following the intelligent and independent Jane, the novel explores themes of social class, love, and self-worth. Brontë's evocative writing brings Jane's struggles and triumphs to life, making you root for her as she navigates a harsh upbringing, finds love in an unexpected place, and grapples with difficult choices. While the Victorian setting might seem distant, Jane's yearning for autonomy and her unwavering spirit resonate with readers today. Be prepared for some melodrama, but also for a powerful love story and a truly unforgettable heroine.







4. It

~ Stephen King

★★★★★

Stephen King's It is a horror epic that sinks its teeth into coming-of-age themes. The story follows the Losers' Club, a group of bullied children in a small town with a big secret: an ancient, shape-shifting evil entity known only as "It" preys on their fears. King weaves a chilling tale where nostalgia for childhood friendships intertwines with the monstrous entity they must confront. It's a hefty read, but King's immersive writing and unforgettable characters make it a worthwhile journey. Be prepared for some disturbing content, but also for a powerful story about facing your fears and the enduring strength of friendship. Recommended for horror fans and those who enjoy dark coming-of- age stories. 







5. The Lord of the Rings

~ J.R.R. Tolkien

★★★★☆

The Lord of the Rings by J.R.R. Tolkien is a monumental work of fantasy that established the genre as we know it. The story follows a hobbit named Frodo on a perilous quest to destroy the One Ring, an artifact of ultimate power coveted by the evil Dark Lord Sauron. Tolkien's meticulously crafted world, Middle-earth, is a rich tapestry of detailed landscapes, unique creatures, and invented languages. While the journey is long, filled with fellowship, peril, and epic battles, it's also a testament to the power of good versus evil, the unassuming courage of hobbits, and the importance of friendship. Be aware, the book is lengthy with a unique writing style, but for those who dive in, it's a rewarding adventure that will stay with you long after the last page. 







6. Romeo and Juliet

~ William Shakespeare

★★★☆☆

Romeo and Juliet by William Shakespeare is a timeless tragedy that continues to resonate with readers centuries after it was written. The star-crossed lovers, Romeo Montague and Juliet Capulet, ignite a passionate romance despite their families' bitter feud. Shakespeare's masterful use of language, including beautiful sonnets and dramatic soliloquies, elevates the story from a simple romance to a profound exploration of love, fate, and the destructive power of hatred. The play is filled with memorable characters, from the fiery Juliet to Mercutio, Romeo's witty friend, and Friar Laurence, the voice of reason amidst the chaos. However, be prepared for the play's tragic nature and the fast-moving plot, which can be challenging for some readers unfamiliar with Shakespeare's Elizabethan English. Overall, Romeo and Juliet is a classic for a reason. It's a powerful story about the enduring power of love, even in the face of overwhelming odds. If you're willing to put in the effort to understand the language, it's a rewarding read.







7. Of Jade and Dragons

~ Amber Chan

★★★☆☆

Of Jade and Dragons by Amber Chen blends Asian fantasy with steampunk elements for a unique and captivating world. Aihui Ying, disguised as a boy, enters an engineering academy to find her father's killer. The novel offers a strong female lead in Ying, who is both intelligent and resourceful. The engineering aspect adds a fresh twist, and the mystery keeps you guessing. Some readers might find the plot predictable, and the world-building, while interesting, could be richer. Overall, Of Jade and Dragons is a fun and fast-paced adventure perfect for fans of YA fantasy with a dash of steampunk and a coming-of-age story. 







8. A Game of Thrones

~ George R. R. Martin

★★★★★

A Game of Thrones, the first book in George R.R. Martin's epic fantasy series "A Song of Ice and Fire," is a gripping introduction to a vast and complex world. We meet noble families vying for power on the Iron Throne of Westeros, while a long-dormant threat stirs beyond the Wall. Martin's masterful storytelling creates a sense of realism through his rich world-building and multi-layered characters. No one is entirely good or bad, and no one is safe from his shocking plot twists. Be prepared for violence, betrayal, and political maneuvering, but also for deep characters and a narrative that will leave you hungry for more. This is a must-read for fans of complex fantasy with a mature edge. However, the sheer size of the commitment and the brutality might not be for everyone. 







9. Amblyopia

~ Ananda Lima

★★★★☆

Amblyopia by Ananda Lima is a poignant and evocative poetry collection that delves into themes of perception, identity, and the complex nuances of seeing and being seen. The title itself, referencing the medical condition often called "lazy eye," sets the stage for a deeper exploration of vision—both literal and metaphorical. Lima's verses are rich with imagery and emotion, capturing the reader's attention with their vivid and often haunting quality. Each poem invites introspection, encouraging readers to reflect on their own experiences and the ways in which they perceive the world. Lima's mastery of language and her ability to convey profound meaning through succinct, powerful lines make Amblyopia a compelling read that lingers in the mind long after the final page is turned. 







10. Daughter of Calamity

~ Rosalie M. Lin

★★★☆☆

Daughter of Calamity by Rosalie M. Lin plunges you into the glittering decadence of 1930s Shanghai. Jingwen, a captivating dancer by night and her grandmother's right hand by day, finds herself embroiled in a dark magical conspiracy. While the story thrives on its unique blend of historical fiction and fantasy, some may find the plot and characters a touch shallow. However, if you're looking for a thrilling adventure with a dash of danger and a whole lot of Shanghai flair, this novel is definitely worth a try. Daughter of Calamity is a good choice for those who enjoy fast-paced fantasy with a strong historical influence. If you're looking for intricate character development, this might not be the perfect fit. 







11. The Six Wives of Henry VIII

~ Alison Weir

★★★★☆

Alison Weir's "The Six Wives of Henry VIII" delves into the dramatic lives of Henry's ill-fated queens, offering a captivating and meticulously researched look beyond the familiar rhyme. Weir breathes life into each woman, Catherine of Aragon, Anne Boleyn, Jane Seymour, Anne of Cleves, Katherine Howard, and Catherine Parr, exploring their personalities, ambitions, and tragic downfalls. This is not just a chronicle of marriages and annulments; it's a journey into the treacherous world of the Tudor court, where alliances could shift overnight and whispers could lead to the executioner's block. Weir expertly weaves together letters, chronicles, and financial records, painting a vivid picture of the political and religious turmoil that shaped this era. However, the book might not be entirely impartial. While Weir sheds light on the complexities faced by Henry's wives, some readers might find his own motivations and psychological state less thoroughly explored. Despite this, "The Six Wives of Henry VIII" remains a captivating read for anyone interested in English history, offering a fresh perspective on a period as dramatic as any fictional tale. 







12. Pride and Prejudice

~ Jane Austen

★★★★☆

A timeless classic, Jane Austen's Pride and Prejudice offers a witty and delightful exploration of love and society in 19th century England. Elizabeth Bennet, our spirited protagonist, is as intelligent as she is independent, refusing to settle for anything less than a love based on mutual respect. Her encounters with the aloof and prideful Mr. Darcy spark a battle of wits that will keep you turning the pages. Austen's sharp observations on social class and the pressures placed on women are as relevant today as they were two centuries ago. While the romance takes center stage, the novel offers much more, with a cast of unforgettable characters and a timeless message about self-discovery and overcoming prejudice. Be prepared to be charmed by Austen's wit and captivated by Elizabeth's journey to love.
